The Southern Sentinel

Space is increasingly congested. Over 35,000 trackable objects are hurtling around Earth at 28,000 km/h. Yet, for decades, humanity has been watching the skies with one eye closed.

The Data Void

Historically, Space Situational Awareness (SSA) infrastructure was concentrated in the Northern Hemisphere—built by the US, Europe, and Russia.

This left a massive blind spot over the Southern Hemisphere. Satellites and lethal debris would disappear from radar as they crossed the equator, leaving operators guessing their exact trajectories.

Australia's Advantage

To prevent cascading orbital collisions (the Kessler Syndrome), continuous tracking is required. Australia's vast landmass provides the perfect anchor for the Southern Hemisphere.

By placing advanced phased-array radars in locations like Western Australia and the Northern Territory, the blind spot is finally illuminated.

Radar Active

Commercial SSA networks now blanket the southern skies. These radars are sensitive enough to detect objects as small as a golf ball in Low Earth Orbit (LEO).

Notice objects turning red as they pass into the southern hemisphere—representing active tracking by radar.
